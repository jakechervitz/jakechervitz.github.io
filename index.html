Bellabeat Marketing Analysis

How can a wellness company play it smart?

Role
Sole analyst — end to end
Tools
Python · pandas · matplotlib · seaborn · Excel · PowerPoint
Context
Google Data Analytics Capstone

Bellabeat makes health-focused smart products for women — an app, a few wearables, and a smart water bottle. The question from their team: what are people actually doing with fitness devices, and what should that mean for our marketing?

I worked through two months of public FitBit data — 35 users, 61 days, 46,000+ hourly observations — cleaned and analyzed it in Python, and turned the findings into five specific, testable marketing recommendations for the Bellabeat app.

What the data said

Five findings, each tied to something Bellabeat could do.

Finding 01

There's no "typical" user.

Users split almost evenly across four activity tiers — 23%, 29%, 23%, 26%. No single tier holds more than 30% of the audience.

One marketing voice can't reach more than a quarter of users well. The data points clearly toward building four distinct personas.
Finding 02

People sit a lot. Like, a lot.

About 79% of the average tracked day is sedentary — that's roughly 26 sedentary minutes for every 1 genuinely active minute. Even the most active users sit most of the day.

The biggest health lever isn't more workouts — it's less sitting. That reframes the entire product message.
Finding 03

Activity peaks at 7 PM, with a clear afternoon lull.

Movement climbs through the day to a 7 PM peak (555 steps), with a visible dip between 1 and 4 PM and a wind-down after 9 PM.

Those quiet windows are exactly when to time notifications: movement nudges at 1–4 PM, bedtime prompts at 9–10 PM.
Finding 04

A third of sleep-trackers get under 6 hours.

Only 46% hit the 7-hour minimum, and 33% average under 6 hours — yet sleep efficiency is high at 92%.

High efficiency + low duration means the problem is bedtime, not biology. That's a behavior you can coach.
Finding 05

Sitting more = sleeping less — the strongest signal in the data.

Sedentary time correlates with poor sleep at −0.64. Steps barely move the needle (−0.20), and very-active minutes even less (−0.09). It's specifically sitting, not lack of fitness, that lines up with sleep loss.

Reducing sitting time could improve fitness and sleep at once — a rare two-for-one for the product story.
Finding 06

Engagement decays fast.

Users logged data for only 39 of a possible 61 days on average, and 11% of days were non-wear days. People drift away from the device quickly after they start.

Retention is its own problem worth solving — a tiered re-engagement flow (nudge at 3 days, email at 7, digest at 14) instead of streak-based shaming.
What I recommended

Five moves for the Bellabeat app.

01

Message by tier

Build content streams for each persona instead of one voice for everyone.

02

Lean into sitting less

Make movement minutes the hero metric — less "track your workout," more "stand up."

03

Time notifications

Movement nudges in the 1–4 PM lull, bedtime prompts at 9–10 PM.

04

Real sleep coaching

A "bedtime drift" feature — the fix is going to bed earlier, not chasing sleep quality.

05

Re-engage lapsed users

Nudge at 3 days, email at 7, digest at 14. Skip the streak-based shaming.

+

Each tied to a metric

Every recommendation comes with a concrete way to measure whether it's working.

What this can't tell us

  • 35 users is a small sample — findings are directional, not statistically airtight.
  • No demographic data, so I can't confirm the dataset matches Bellabeat's women-focused audience.
  • Data is from 2016 — pre-pandemic, before remote work reshaped sitting habits.
  • Only 69% of users logged sleep, so sleep findings likely skew toward the more health-engaged.
  • No revenue data — recommendations rest on engagement logic, not direct revenue modeling.

How I worked

  • Investigated duplicates instead of dropping them — 24 April-12 records turned out to be a data-extraction cutoff, not true dupes.
  • Filtered non-wear days (under 100 steps) so they didn't drag down activity averages — the 11% non-wear rate became a finding itself.
  • Used research-backed Tudor-Locke step thresholds for activity tiers, not arbitrary cutoffs.
  • Documented every cleaning decision so the analysis is reproducible from the notebook.
  • Completed with AI assistance for code structure and writing polish; all analytical decisions, code, and validation were my own.
